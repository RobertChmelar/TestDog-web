Your puppy's first day home: a complete checklist

Updated July 20, 2026 · reading time approx. 6 min

Bringing a puppy home is a big deal — and it's also the day you'll make the most mistakes if you're unprepared. The good news: you can prepare. This checklist walks you through everything from the drive home to the first night.

Before you leave to pick up the puppy: get your home ready

A puppy arrives in an unfamiliar place. The less you improvise, the calmer the first hours will be. Set these up in advance:

  • A bed or crate in a quiet corner, away from drafts — this will be the puppy's safe retreat.
  • Food and water bowls — stainless steel or ceramic, easy to clean.
  • The same food the breeder was feeding — a sudden diet change will cause an upset stomach.
  • A playpen or exercise pen — a safe zone where the puppy can't harm itself or your belongings.
  • One or two toys — a flood of new toys is overwhelming; keep it simple.
  • Puppy pads as a backup for indoor accidents.

Puppy-proof the space: tuck away charging cables, shoes, houseplants, and cleaning products.

The car ride: low stress

This is the puppy's first time away from its siblings and mother. Keep it calm:

  • Ask the breeder for a cloth or toy with familiar scents.
  • Have someone sit in the back seat with the puppy — don't let it ride alone in a crate the whole way.
  • Drive smoothly; turn down the radio.
  • On a long trip, stop to offer water.

First hours home: let it explore

Everyone's excited — and the puppy is hiding under the sofa. That's completely normal. Key rules for the first few hours:

  • Go outside first — before entering the house, give the puppy a few minutes in the yard or a grassy area. If it pees outside, praise warmly.
  • Let it explore at its own pace — don't pull it out of hiding, don't force cuddles.
  • Limit visitors — today is not the day to invite friends over.
  • Keep noise down — TV on low, calmer-than-usual atmosphere.

If you have young children, set ground rules now: quiet voices, sit on the floor instead of chasing, no grabbing.

Feeding on day one

Find out from the breeder exactly what the puppy ate and how often. Ideally, give the exact same food at the same times. Moving house is stressful enough — don't add a food change on top of it. A soft stool is likely regardless; you don't want to make it worse.

After every meal, take the puppy outside within 10–15 minutes.

Toilet training: laying the foundation

An 8-week-old puppy can't hold its bladder for more than 2–3 hours. You won't have a house-trained dog by tonight, but day one sets the pattern:

  • Right after waking up → outside immediately.
  • After every meal → outside within 15 minutes.
  • After play → outside.
  • Every 2 hours → outside as a precaution.

When the puppy goes outside, praise immediately and enthusiastically. If it has an accident indoors, don't scold — quietly clean it up and be faster next time.

The first night: what to expect

The puppy will probably cry. It's used to sleeping in a pile with its siblings. You have two solid options — pick one and stick with it:

  • Bed next to yours — the puppy can smell and hear you, settles faster. Set an alarm to take it outside once during the night.
  • Crate in another room — it will whine the first night or two, then learn to settle. Don't go back when it cries — that teaches it crying works.

Both approaches work. What doesn't work: responding to every whimper — that guarantees a week of sleepless nights.

The bottom line

Day one calls for patience more than excitement. Your puppy doesn't need a flood of toys or non-stop attention — it needs calm, consistency, and the sense that this new place is safe. The quieter you are, the faster it settles.
